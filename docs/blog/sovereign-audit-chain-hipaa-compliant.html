HIPAA-Compliant Audit Chain
in the Skr8tr Conductor

Cryptographically chained audit ledger, AES-256-GCM at-rest encryption, and RFC 5424 syslog forwarding — built directly into the enterprise Conductor. No external database. No log aggregator. No vendor lock-in.

Systems engineer — C23, Rust, NixOS, post-quantum security

Every regulated industry — healthcare, finance, payment processing — demands an immutable record of who did what and when. HIPAA calls it an "audit control." PCI DSS calls it "audit trail." NIST 800-53 calls it the AU family of controls. SOC 2 calls it CC7.2. The requirement is always the same: capture every privileged action in a tamper-evident log, encrypt it at rest, ship it to a SIEM, and be able to prove the chain was not modified.

The standard answer to this problem involves Elasticsearch, Logstash, Filebeat, Kibana, and six YAML configuration files. We built equivalent capability into Skr8tr's enterprise Conductor — no external processes, no moving parts, no recurring SaaS bill.

Why compliance logging is non-negotiable

If you process PHI (Protected Health Information), you are subject to HIPAA's Security Rule, specifically 45 CFR § 164.312(b): "Implement hardware, software, and/or procedural mechanisms that record and examine activity in information systems that contain or use electronic protected health information." The penalty for a breach involving missing audit trails: up to $1.9M per violation category per year.

PCI DSS 10.2 requires logging all access to cardholder data and all administrative actions. PCI DSS 10.5.3 requires protecting audit logs from destruction and unauthorized modification. SOC 2 CC7.2 requires that system events be logged and that the logs be reviewed for anomalies. NIST 800-53 AU-9 requires protection of audit information and AU-10 requires non-repudiation — the ability to prove that a specific entity performed a specific action.

None of these frameworks care how you implement the logging. They care about three properties: completeness (every relevant action is captured), integrity (the log cannot be altered after the fact), and confidentiality (sensitive log data is encrypted at rest).

The Skr8tr enterprise audit ledger satisfies all three properties without any external infrastructure. The chain lives inside the Conductor process. The encryption key never touches the log file. SIEM forwarding runs over your existing network.

What the audit chain provides

Every privileged action through the Conductor is written to a cryptographically chained ledger. Each entry includes the source identity of the operator, the application target, a timestamp, and a hash that chains it to every entry before it. Any attempt to modify, delete, or reorder entries breaks the chain — detectable in milliseconds with a full chain verification command.

At-rest encryption is provided by AES-256-GCM with a fresh random IV per entry — no IV is ever reused. The encryption key is stored separately from the log file and is never written to disk alongside the entries it protects.

Every audit event is simultaneously forwarded to your SIEM over RFC 5424 structured syslog, with all fields available as structured key-value data for automatic extraction. Both UDP and TLS/TCP transports are supported. Tested against Splunk, Graylog, IBM QRadar, Microsoft Sentinel, and Elastic/ELK.
